## What I'm building

Provenance Guard is a backend a creative-writing platform could plug in to answer
one question about a submitted piece of text: *was this written by a person, or
generated by AI and passed off as human?* It doesn't police creativity — it
classifies content, scores how sure it is, hands the reader a plain-language
transparency label, and gives the creator a way to appeal when we get it wrong.

The honest framing up front: **perfect AI detection is an unsolved problem.** I'm
not pretending to solve it. The engineering challenge here is being honest about
uncertainty and giving creators a fair path to push back — not chasing an
accuracy number I can't actually hit.

### The one bias that drives every decision

On a *writing* platform, a false positive — labeling a human's real work as
AI-generated — is worse than a false negative. Accuse a real writer of cheating
and you've insulted them and possibly hurt their reputation; miss one AI post and
a reader sees slightly-less-context. So the whole system leans the same way:
**when in doubt, say "uncertain," not "AI."** You'll see that asymmetry show up
in the thresholds, the label wording, and the appeals flow.

## The path a submission takes (narrative)

Plain English, start to finish — this is the thing I'll trace every feature back to:

1. A creator (or the platform on their behalf) `POST`s text + a `creator_id` to **`/submit`**.
2. The text hits **Signal 1 (LLM judge)** — Groq reads it and returns a probability that it's AI-written, plus a one-line rationale.
3. The same text hits **Signal 2 (stylometric heuristics)** — pure-Python stats turn structural properties into a second AI-probability.
4. The **confidence scorer** combines the two signals into one `ai_likelihood`, decides the **verdict** (Likely AI / Uncertain / Likely human), and computes a **confidence** in that verdict.
5. The **label generator** maps the verdict to one of three plain-language **transparency labels**.
6. The **audit log** records everything — both signal scores, the combined score, the verdict, the label, a timestamp, status `classified`.
7. The response goes back: `content_id`, attribution, confidence, both signal scores, and the label text.

Later, if the creator disagrees:

8. They `POST` the `content_id` + their reasoning to **`/appeal`**.
9. The system flips that content's status to `under_review`, writes a second audit entry that sits next to the original decision, and returns a confirmation. No automated re-classification — a human owns the re-decision.

**Narrative:** the submission flow fans one piece of text out to two independent
signals, funnels them back into a single calibrated score, turns that into a
human-readable label, and logs the whole thing before responding. The appeal flow
is deliberately small — it doesn't re-run detection, it just flips status to
`under_review` and drops an appeal entry next to the original decision so a human
reviewer can see both side by side.

## Detection signals

I need at least two, and they have to be *genuinely distinct* — capturing
different properties of the text, not two flavors of the same idea. Mine are one
**semantic** signal and one **structural** signal. That's the point: when a
holistic LLM read and a cold statistical read *agree*, that's real evidence; when
they *disagree*, that disagreement is itself information (and it pushes me toward
"uncertain").

### Signal 1 — LLM judge (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)

- **What it measures:** holistic semantic + stylistic coherence. I prompt the
  model as a judge ("estimate the probability this text was AI-generated, 0.00–1.00,
  and give a one-line reason"), then parse out the number. This is the *LLM-as-judge*
  pattern — the output feeds my pipeline, not the user.
- **Output shape:** `p_llm ∈ [0,1]` = P(text is AI), plus a short rationale string I log.
- **Why it's useful:** it's the only signal that "reads" the text the way a person
  would — catching the tell-tale flatness of generic AI prose, the over-hedged
  "it is important to note" register, the suspiciously even-handed structure.
- **Its blind spots (if I can't name these I don't understand the signal):**
  - It's confidently wrong sometimes — AI detection isn't something the model is actually reliable at, so I treat `p_llm` as one noisy vote, not gospel.
  - It penalizes formal/uniform *human* writing (academic, ESL, technical) the same way it penalizes AI. Classic false-positive trap.
  - It can be talked around — lightly-edited AI text reads "human enough" and scores lower than it should.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python, no external libs)

- **What it measures:** structural statistics that tend to differ between human and
  AI writing. AI text is *uniform*; human writing is *bursty* and irregular. I
  compute three sub-metrics and fold them into one score:
  1. **Sentence-length variance (burstiness)** — humans swing between short and long sentences; AI clusters around a comfortable medium. Low variance → more AI-like.
  2. **Type-token ratio (vocabulary diversity)** — unique words ÷ total words. Very smooth, mid-range diversity reads AI-ish; humans are spikier (slang, repetition, odd word choices).
  3. **"AI-tell" transition density** — rate of connective/hedge phrases AI overuses ("furthermore," "moreover," "it is important to note," "in conclusion," "delve," "in today's world"). High density → more AI-like.
- **Output shape:** `p_style ∈ [0,1]` = P(text is AI), a weighted blend of the three normalized sub-metrics.
- **Why it's useful:** it's cheap, deterministic, has no idea what the text *means*,
  and can't be sweet-talked. It's a completely different failure surface from the LLM.
- **Its blind spots:**
  - **Short text breaks it.** Variance and type-token ratio are meaningless on 30 words. Under a length floor I down-weight this signal hard (see edge cases).
  - **Formal human prose scores AI-like** — academic and ESL writing is uniform and transition-heavy by nature. Same false-positive trap as Signal 1, for a different reason.
  - **Poetry / experimental writing breaks the assumptions** — heavy repetition + simple vocabulary tank the type-token ratio and look "uniform" even when a human clearly wrote it.

### How I combine them

Weighted average, LLM trusted a bit more because it actually reads meaning:

```
raw = 0.65 · p_llm + 0.35 · p_style
```

Stylometrics gets the smaller weight because it's noisier on short and on formal
text — the two cases I most want to avoid false-positiving. But it still gets a
real vote, and crucially it can *veto confidence* through the disagreement rule
below.

## Uncertainty representation (confidence scoring)

This is the heart of the project, and it's a **design decision before it's a
technical one.** I decided what I want the numbers to *mean* first:

> **"ai_likelihood"** = how AI-ish the combined evidence is (0 = certainly human, 1 = certainly AI).
> **"confidence"** = how sure I am of the *verdict I'm putting in front of a reader.* High only when the evidence is strong **and** both signals agree.

### What 0.6 means

A `raw` of 0.6 means "leaning AI, but not by enough to say so out loud." It lands
in the **Uncertain** band on purpose. I never want a reader to see "AI-generated"
off the back of a coin-flip-plus-a-nudge. 0.6 is exactly the kind of score that
should produce a *meaningfully* softer label than 0.95 — that's the whole point of
having three bands instead of a binary flip at 0.5.

### The scoring rule (this is what the code implements)

```python
p_llm, p_style          # each ∈ [0,1] = P(text is AI)
raw      = 0.65*p_llm + 0.35*p_style
disagree = abs(p_llm - p_style)

# --- verdict ---
if   disagree > 0.40:   verdict = "uncertain"        # signals fight → never accuse
elif raw >= 0.75:       verdict = "likely_ai"
elif raw <= 0.40:       verdict = "likely_human"
else:                   verdict = "uncertain"

# --- confidence in that verdict (0–1) ---
strength   = abs(raw - 0.5) * 2          # 0 at the fence, 1 at the extremes
confidence = round(strength * (1 - disagree), 2)
```

### The thresholds, and the asymmetry baked into them

| Band            | `raw` range | Width | Verdict        |
|-----------------|-------------|-------|----------------|
| Likely human    | 0.00 – 0.40 | 0.40  | `likely_human` |
| Uncertain       | 0.40 – 0.75 | 0.35  | `uncertain`    |
| Likely AI       | 0.75 – 1.00 | 0.25  | `likely_ai`    |

Two deliberate choices here, both serving the false-positive bias:

1. **The "Likely AI" band is the narrowest (0.25).** It takes a `raw` of 0.75+ to
   accuse someone. The "Likely human" band is the widest (0.40). It is *structurally
   easier* to be cleared than to be accused.
2. **The disagreement override only ever pushes toward "uncertain."** If the two
   signals are more than 0.40 apart, I refuse to give a confident verdict in either
   direction — but in practice this matters most for *blocking* a false AI accusation
   when, say, the LLM is suspicious but the stylometrics say "human." Disagreement
   can never *create* an AI verdict; it can only dissolve one.

### How I'll validate the scores are actually meaningful

Not "trust me" — I'll run the four calibration inputs from the spec (clearly AI,
clearly human, formal-human borderline, lightly-edited-AI borderline) and check
that:
- the clearly-AI and clearly-human cases land in opposite confident bands,
- the two borderline cases land in **Uncertain** with visibly *lower* confidence,
- and if any score contradicts my intuition, I print `p_llm` and `p_style`
  separately to find which signal is misbehaving before I touch the weights.

Worked numbers for those inputs are in the README's confidence-scoring section.

## Transparency label design

Three variants. Rules I'm writing to: **plain language, no jargon** (no "classifier
output," no "logit," no "score of 0.74" as the headline), and the **text itself has
to change** between confident and uncertain — not just a number. Every label that
could be an accusation also names the appeal path, because of the false-positive bias.

### Variant 1 — High-confidence AI (`likely_ai`)

> 🤖 **Likely AI-generated.** Our analysis suggests this text was most likely
> produced by an AI tool. Two independent checks — a language-model review and a
> writing-style analysis — strongly agreed. No automated check is perfect. **If you
> wrote this yourself, you can appeal and a person will review it.**

### Variant 2 — High-confidence human (`likely_human`)

> ✍️ **Likely written by a person.** Our analysis suggests this text was most
> likely written by a human. Both of our checks pointed the same way. This is our
> best estimate, not a guarantee.

### Variant 3 — Uncertain (`uncertain`)

> 🤔 **Inconclusive.** We couldn't confidently tell whether this was written by a
> person or generated by AI — our two checks either disagreed or landed in the
> middle. We're showing this as uncertain rather than guessing. Treat it as "not
> enough signal to call."

## Appeals workflow

- **Who can appeal:** the creator of the content (matched on `creator_id` from the
  original submission). In a real deployment this would be auth-gated; here it's
  open for grading visibility.
- **What they provide:** the `content_id` from their `/submit` response + a free-text
  `creator_reasoning` ("I wrote this myself — I'm a non-native English speaker and my
  style reads formal").
- **What the system does on receipt:**
  1. Looks up the original decision by `content_id`.
  2. Flips that content's status from `classified` → `under_review`.
  3. Appends a **new** audit entry (it does *not* overwrite the original) carrying the appeal reasoning, the original verdict, and a timestamp — so the two sit together in the log.
  4. Returns a confirmation. **No automated re-classification** — a human owns the re-decision.
- **What a reviewer sees in the queue:** every entry with `status == "under_review"`,
  showing the original verdict + confidence, *both* individual signal scores, the
  label that was shown, the creator's reasoning, and the timestamps. The individual
  signal scores are the useful part — a reviewer can immediately see "ah, the LLM
  flagged it but stylometrics said human, that's why it was borderline."

## Anticipated edge cases (where this thing will get it wrong)

Naming these specifically, because "it might be inaccurate" isn't a real answer.

1. **The non-native English writer.** ESL writing is often formal, grammatically
   careful, and structurally uniform — which is *exactly* what both my signals read
   as "AI." Stylometrics sees low burstiness + high transition density; the LLM sees
   that flat, hedged register. This is my highest-stakes false-positive case, and
   it's a real person being told a machine thinks they cheated. It's also the
   archetypal appeal — the workflow exists largely for them.

2. **The repetitive / simple-vocabulary poem.** A poem leaning on refrains and plain
   words tanks the type-token ratio and flattens sentence-length variance, so
   stylometrics screams "AI." The LLM *might* save it (it can recognize verse), which
   is precisely the disagreement case my scoring is built to catch → it should land
   in **Uncertain** rather than a false accusation. Good test of the override.

3. **Very short submissions (< ~50 words).** Stylometric stats are statistically
   meaningless on a tweet-length input. Plan: enforce a length floor; below it, hard
   down-weight `p_style` and lean almost entirely on the LLM (and bias toward
   Uncertain). Don't pretend a variance computed over three sentences means anything.

4. **Lightly-edited AI text.** Someone runs an AI draft through a couple of human
   tweaks. This is *genuinely* ambiguous — it's part human, part machine — and I
   think the honest answer is "Uncertain," which is where mid-range `raw` puts it.
   I'm calling this a feature, not a bug.

## API surface (the contract everything else implements)

| Method | Path       | Accepts                                   | Returns |
|--------|------------|-------------------------------------------|---------|
| POST   | `/submit`  | `{ "text": str, "creator_id": str }`      | `{ content_id, attribution, confidence, ai_likelihood, signals: {llm_score, style_score}, label, status }` |
| POST   | `/appeal`  | `{ "content_id": str, "creator_reasoning": str }` | `{ content_id, status: "under_review", message }` |
| GET    | `/log`     | —                                          | `{ entries: [ ...most recent audit records... ] }` |

`content_id` is a UUID minted at submission. It's the join key between `/submit`,
`/appeal`, and the audit log — everything hangs off it.

## Audit log schema

Append-only **JSONL** (one JSON object per line). I'm using JSONL over SQLite
because it's append-only by nature, survives a corrupted line, and is the format I
already used in Lab 4 — no reason to add a database for this scale. A classification
entry:

```json
{
  "content_id": "3f7a2b1e-...",
  "creator_id": "test-user-1",
  "timestamp": "2026-06-28T14:32:10.123Z",
  "attribution": "likely_ai",
  "ai_likelihood": 0.91,
  "confidence": 0.78,
  "llm_score": 0.92,
  "style_score": 0.88,
  "signals_used": ["llm_judge", "stylometrics"],
  "label_shown": "Likely AI-generated. ...",
  "status": "classified",
  "appeal_reasoning": null
}
```

An appeal appends a second line for the same `content_id` with
`"status": "under_review"` and `appeal_reasoning` populated. The original line
stays untouched — that's the accountability story.

## Rate limiting plan

`Flask-Limiter`, in-memory storage (`storage_uri="memory://"`), applied to `/submit`:

```
@limiter.limit("10 per minute;100 per day")
```

Reasoning (the numbers aren't arbitrary):
- **A real writer is bursty but low-volume.** Someone polishing one piece might
  resubmit a handful of times in a few minutes — `10/min` covers that comfortably.
- **Nobody legitimately submits 100 distinct pieces a day.** `100/day` is a generous
  ceiling for even a prolific creator while cutting off a script trying to
  reverse-engineer the classifier or flood the log.
- **Every `/submit` costs a Groq call.** Latency + free-tier quota. Capping requests
  protects the API budget as much as it protects the system.
