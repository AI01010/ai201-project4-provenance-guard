# Provenance Guard

A backend a creative-writing platform can plug in to ask one question about
submitted text: **was this written by a person, or generated by AI?** It runs two
independent detection signals, combines them into a calibrated confidence score,
shows the reader a plain-language transparency label, lets creators appeal, and
logs every decision. Built on Flask + Groq.

The design is opinionated about one thing: on a writing platform, **falsely
accusing a real writer is worse than missing an AI post.** Everything below leans
that way — high bar to accuse, wide "uncertain" band, and an appeal path that's a
first-class feature, not an afterthought. Full design rationale is in
[planning.md](planning.md).

> **AI detection is an unsolved problem and I'm not pretending otherwise.** The
> interesting engineering here isn't a magic accuracy number, it's communicating
> uncertainty honestly and giving creators a way to push back.

## Architecture overview — the path a submission takes

```
text + creator_id ──► POST /submit
                          │
              ┌───────────┴───────────┐
              ▼                       ▼
   Signal 1: LLM judge       Signal 2: stylometrics
   (Groq llama-3.3-70b)      (pure Python)
        p_llm ∈[0,1]            p_style ∈[0,1]
              └───────────┬───────────┘
                          ▼
              Confidence scorer
        raw = 0.65·p_llm + 0.35·p_style
        + disagreement override → verdict + confidence
                          ▼
              Transparency label (1 of 3 variants)
                          ▼
              Audit log (append-only JSONL)  ──►  JSON response
```

A submission fans out to two independent signals (one semantic, one structural),
funnels back into a single score, becomes a reader-facing label, and gets logged
before the response goes out. An appeal (`POST /appeal`) doesn't re-run
detection — it flips the content's status to `under_review` and appends an appeal
entry next to the original decision so a human reviewer sees both side by side.

### API endpoints

| Method | Path      | Body                                          | Returns |
|--------|-----------|-----------------------------------------------|---------|
| POST   | `/submit` | `{text, creator_id, content_type?}`           | content_id, attribution, confidence, both signal scores, label |
| POST   | `/appeal` | `{content_id, creator_reasoning}`             | status `under_review` + confirmation |
| GET    | `/log`    | `?limit=N`                                     | recent audit entries |
| GET    | `/stats`  | —                                              | verdict mix, appeal rate, avg confidence (analytics) |

## Detection signals

Two signals, deliberately measuring **different** properties. When a holistic read
and a cold statistical read agree, that's real evidence; when they disagree, the
disagreement itself becomes a reason to say "uncertain."

### Signal 1 — LLM judge (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)
- **Measures:** holistic semantic + stylistic coherence. Prompted as a judge to
  return `P(AI)` plus a one-line reason (the LLM-as-judge pattern — output feeds
  the pipeline, not the user).
- **Why:** it's the only signal that "reads" the text — catching generic flatness,
  the over-hedged "it is important to note" register, suspiciously even structure.
- **What it misses:** it's confidently wrong sometimes (detection isn't reliable,
  so I treat it as one noisy vote); it penalizes formal / ESL human writing the
  same way it penalizes AI; lightly-edited AI slips under it.

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python, no libraries)
- **Measures:** structure, via three sub-metrics blended into one `P(AI)`:
  burstiness (sentence-length variance — AI is uniform), type-token ratio
  (vocabulary diversity), and AI-tell phrase density ("furthermore," "delve,"
  "it is important to note," etc.).
- **Why:** cheap, deterministic, has no idea what the text means, and can't be
  sweet-talked — a totally different failure surface from the LLM.
- **What it misses:** short text breaks the stats (flagged unreliable under 50
  words → stylometrics down-weighted); formal/ESL prose scores AI-like; repetitive
  poetry tanks the type-token ratio and looks "AI."

## Confidence scoring

Two numbers, with distinct meanings I decided on **before** writing the math:

- **`ai_likelihood`** — how AI-ish the combined evidence is (0 human … 1 AI).
- **`confidence`** — how sure I am of the *verdict shown to a reader.* High only
  when evidence is strong **and** both signals agree.

```python
raw      = 0.65*p_llm + 0.35*p_style       # LLM weighted higher; it reads meaning
disagree = abs(p_llm - p_style)

if   disagree > 0.40:  verdict = "uncertain"     # signals fight → never accuse
elif raw >= 0.70:      verdict = "likely_ai"      # narrow band (high bar to accuse)
elif raw <= 0.40:      verdict = "likely_human"   # wide band (easy to be cleared)
else:                  verdict = "uncertain"

strength   = abs(raw - 0.5) * 2
confidence = round(strength * (1 - disagree), 2)
```

The asymmetry is the whole point: the `likely_ai` band is narrow, the
`likely_human` band is wide, and **signal disagreement can only ever push toward
"uncertain" — never toward an accusation.**

### Two real submissions with different confidence

Captured by `scripts/capture_evidence.py` (real Groq calls):

| Submission | p_llm | p_style | ai_likelihood | verdict | **confidence** |
|------------|------:|--------:|--------------:|---------|---------------:|
| Casual human (a ramen review) | 0.20 | 0.12 | 0.17 | `likely_human` | **0.60** |
| Formal human (an econ paragraph) | 0.80 | 0.44 | 0.75 | `likely_ai` ⚠️ | **0.32** |

The casual review reads decisively human (both signals agree, confidence 0.60).
The formal econ paragraph is the hard case — the LLM thinks it's AI (0.80), the
stylometrics is lukewarm (0.44), confidence drops to 0.32. **That second one is a
false positive**, and it's the appeal example below. More on why in Known
Limitations.

### Did I validate the scores are meaningful?

Yes — I built a real labeled dataset and measured, instead of eyeballing.
`data/gather_data.py` scrapes **24 human samples** (PoetryDB public-domain poetry,
Wikipedia intros, Art Institute open-access descriptions) and generates **19 AI
samples** with Groq (poems, blog posts, marketing, encyclopedic, art labels).
`scripts/evaluate.py` runs all 43 through the pipeline. Results
(`data/evaluation_results.json`):

```
coverage (made a confident call):     46.5%
decisive accuracy (when it called):   75.0%
FALSE POSITIVES (human → AI):         0  (0.0% of the 24 human samples)
false negatives (AI → human):         5  (26.3% of AI)

confusion matrix (rows = truth, cols = verdict):
            likely_human   uncertain   likely_ai
  human              13          11           0
  ai                  5          12           2
```

The honest read: the system **never once falsely accused a human in the eval set**
(my #1 goal), but it's *deeply* cautious — it abstains ("uncertain") on more than
half of everything, and it only confidently caught AI twice. It is, by design, a
system that would rather shrug than wrongly accuse. (Small sample, 43 items —
results are directional, like a smoke test, not a benchmark.)

## Transparency label

Three variants. Rules: plain language (no "classifier," "logit," "score"), the
*text* changes with confidence (not just the number), and any label that could be
an accusation names the appeal path. Exact text the system emits:

| Verdict | Label text |
|---------|------------|
| **High-confidence AI** | 🤖 **Likely AI-generated.** Our analysis suggests this text was most likely produced by an AI tool. Two independent checks — a language-model review and a writing-style analysis — pointed the same way. *We're fairly confident in this estimate.* No automated check is perfect. If you wrote this yourself, you can appeal and a person will review it. (confidence N%) |
| **High-confidence human** | ✍️ **Likely written by a person.** Our analysis suggests this text was most likely written by a human. Both of our checks pointed the same way. *We're fairly confident in this estimate.* This is our best estimate, not a guarantee. (confidence N%) |
| **Uncertain** | 🤔 **Inconclusive.** We couldn't confidently tell whether this was written by a person or generated by AI — our two checks either disagreed or landed in the middle. We're showing this as uncertain rather than guessing. Treat it as "not enough signal to call." (confidence N%) |

The italic confidence sentence swaps with the band — *"We're fairly confident"*
(high), *"only moderately confident"* (medium), *"Our confidence … is low"* (low) —
so the same verdict at different confidence levels genuinely reads differently.

## Rate limiting

`Flask-Limiter`, in-memory storage, on `/submit`: **`10 per minute; 100 per day`**
per client IP.

Reasoning — the numbers aren't arbitrary:
- A real writer is **bursty but low-volume**: someone polishing one piece might
  resubmit a handful of times in a few minutes. `10/min` covers that.
- **Nobody legitimately submits 100 distinct pieces a day.** `100/day` is generous
  for a prolific creator while cutting off a script flooding the system or trying
  to reverse-engineer the classifier.
- **Every `/submit` costs a Groq call** (latency + free-tier quota), so capping
  protects the API budget as much as the system.

Evidence — a 12-request burst (`scripts/capture_evidence.py`):

```
request  1–7 : 200
request  8–12: 429   ← rate limit kicked in after 7 (3 earlier submits used the window)
status codes : [200, 200, 200, 200, 200, 200, 200, 429, 429, 429, 429, 429]
```

A 429 returns clean JSON, not an HTML error page:

```json
{ "error": "Rate limit exceeded.", "limit": "10 per minute;100 per day",
  "message": "Slow down — you've hit the submission rate limit. Try again shortly." }
```

Append-only **JSONL** at `logs/audit.jsonl` — one object per line, original
decisions never rewritten, an appeal appends a *new* line next to the original.
A committed sample lives at [data/sample_audit_log.jsonl](data/sample_audit_log.jsonl).

classification it contests (same `content_id`, line 3).

## Appeals workflow

The creator sends `content_id` + `creator_reasoning` to `POST /appeal`. The system
looks up the original decision, flips status to `under_review`, appends the appeal
to the log (the original line is never rewritten), and confirms. A reviewer's queue
is every entry with `status == "under_review"`, showing the original verdict, both
signal scores, and the creator's reasoning. A human still owns the final call.

### Appeal re-evaluation (diminishing-returns reweighting)

An appeal isn't just a status flag, it's new information. So `/appeal` re-runs
detection with the creator's reasoning handed to the LLM judge as context, and the
judge is told to stay skeptical: a plausible account of *why* human writing looks
AI-like (non-native English, formal training, heavy editing) can lower the score,
but a bare "I wrote this, trust me" must not. That re-evaluation is then blended
with the original by a **trust weight that grows on a log curve and saturates**:

```
trust(k) = 0.70 * ln(1 + k) / ln(1 + 4)     # k = appeal number, capped at 4
revised_ai_likelihood = original*(1 - trust) + reevaluated*trust
```

Three properties make this safe instead of gameable:
- **Diminishing returns.** The first appeal earns the most benefit-of-the-doubt; each later one earns less.
- **Capped below 1.0.** Trust tops out at 0.70, so an appeal can *never* single-handedly flip a verdict. A human reviewer owns the rest.
- **Empty appeals do nothing.** If the explanation doesn't actually lower the re-evaluated score, the blend doesn't move, no matter how many times you appeal.

Real evidence (`scripts/appeal_demo.py`, live Groq) appealing the formal-human
false positive with the same genuine ESL explanation each time:

| stage | trust | revised ai_likelihood | verdict | confidence |
|-------|------:|----------------------:|---------|-----------:|
| original | — | 0.7467 | `likely_ai` ⚠️ | 0.32 |
| appeal #1 | 0.30 | 0.7211 | `likely_ai` | 0.33 |
| appeal #2 | 0.48 | 0.7061 | `likely_ai` | 0.31 |
| appeal #3 | 0.60 | 0.6955 | `uncertain` | 0.29 |
| appeal #4 | 0.70 | 0.6872 | `uncertain` | 0.28 |

The wrong `likely_ai` accusation relaxes to `uncertain` by the third appeal, with
visibly shrinking steps (0.026, 0.015, 0.011, 0.008) that saturate at the cap. It
deliberately stops at `uncertain` rather than force-flipping to `likely_human`,
because confirming a human is a human is the reviewer's job, not the system's.

## Known limitations

**Formal and non-native-English (ESL) writing is where this breaks — and I can
show it.** Both signals read uniform, careful, transition-heavy prose as AI:
stylometrics sees low burstiness, and the LLM sees the same flat register it
associates with generated text. My own evidence has a live example — the
monetary-policy paragraph (`ai_likelihood` 0.75) was written by a human but got
labeled `likely_ai`. This isn't a bug I can fully patch with these two signals;
formal-human and AI text genuinely overlap in score-space (you can see it in
`data/evaluation_results.json` — human encyclopedic samples score 0.64–0.71, right
on top of the AI samples). It's the single biggest reason the appeal workflow
exists and why the `likely_ai` band sits behind a high bar.

Two more, briefly: **very short text** (< 50 words) makes the stylometric stats
meaningless, so the system down-weights them and leans on the LLM; and
**lightly-edited AI** is genuinely ambiguous and tends to land `uncertain` or tip
to `likely_human` at low confidence — the "less bad" error, on purpose.

## Spec reflection

**Where the spec helped:** writing the confidence rule in
[planning.md](planning.md) — the exact `raw`/`disagreement`/threshold math — before
any code meant the scorer, the labels, and the tests all implemented the *same*
definition of "confidence." There was no moment of "wait, what does 0.6 mean
here?" because I'd already decided.

**Where I diverged, and why:** the spec set `AI_THRESHOLD = 0.75`. After building
the evaluation I found that at 0.75 the system **never confidently caught AI** on
naturalistic text — it routed all 19 AI samples to `uncertain` or `likely_human`.
So I lowered it to **0.70**, which recovered real true positives with zero false
positives *on the eval set*. The catch I then discovered: at 0.70 it false-positives
on harder formal/ESL writing (the appeal example). That's the unavoidable tension
the spec didn't anticipate — there's no threshold in the 0.63–0.75 overlap zone
that separates formal humans from AI cleanly. I shipped 0.70 and leaned harder on
the appeal path rather than pretending the line is clean. (I kept planning.md at
0.75 — it's the record of what I planned; this is the record of what the data
made me do.)

## AI usage

I used an AI coding tool (Claude) to generate code from sections of my
[planning.md](planning.md), then reviewed and overrode it. Two specific instances:

1. **Stylometrics + scoring.** I gave it my detection-signals and
   uncertainty-representation sections and asked for the stylometric metrics and
   the signal-combination function. It produced reasonable metric code, but I
   **overrode the calibration**: it had no real cutoffs, so I tuned the burstiness
   and type-token mappings against my actual dataset, and I **reverted its
   threshold choice** after the evaluation showed 0.75 caught no AI (the 0.75 → 0.70
   divergence above was my call, made from the eval numbers, not the model's).

2. **Data gathering + endpoints.** I asked it to write the scrapers (PoetryDB,
   Wikipedia, Art Institute) and the Flask routes. I **rewrote the failure
   behavior**: I made every signal *fail safe to `uncertain`* (a 0.5 on any LLM
   error) rather than crashing or defaulting to a guess — because on a writing
   platform, failing into an accusation is unacceptable. I also added the
   disagreement override myself; the generated scorer was a plain weighted average
   that would happily accuse on one strong signal.

## Stretch features

- **Multi-modal (second content type).** `/submit` accepts
  `content_type: "art_description"`, and the dataset + evaluation include real art
  descriptions (8 human from the Art Institute of Chicago open-access API, 3
  AI-generated). They flow through the same pipeline and get the same verdict +
  label. *Scope note:* this handles **text about images** (descriptions /
  metadata), not raw image pixels — I didn't have an image-generation API
  (Firefly / Nano Banana) wired up in this environment, and both signals are
  text-based, so descriptions/metadata is the honest extent of the multi-modal
  support.
- **Analytics (`GET /stats`).** Returns ≥3 metrics: verdict distribution
  (AI/human/uncertain ratio), appeal rate, and average confidence across all
  classifications.

Not attempted: ensemble (3rd signal) and provenance certificate — noted as future
work in [planning.md](planning.md).
